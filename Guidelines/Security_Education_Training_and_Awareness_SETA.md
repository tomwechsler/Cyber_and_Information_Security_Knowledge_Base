# Security Education, Training, and Awareness: Building a Human-Centered Cybersecurity Culture

Cybersecurity is often discussed in terms of technology: firewalls, endpoint protection, SIEM platforms, identity systems, vulnerability scanners, and encryption.

But even the best technology can fail when people do not understand security, when processes are unclear, or when behavior does not support the organization’s security goals.

This is why **Security Education, Training, and Awareness**, often referred to as **SETA**, is a critical component of every mature cybersecurity program.

SETA is not just about annual compliance training.

It is about building a culture where people understand risks, know what to do, and behave in ways that actively protect the organization.

In modern cybersecurity, people are not only a risk factor.

They are also one of the most important lines of defense.

## What Is SETA?

**SETA** stands for:

- **Security Education**
- **Security Training**
- **Security Awareness**

These three areas are closely related, but they are not the same.

A mature cybersecurity learning program must understand the difference.

### Security Education

Education focuses on **why** security matters.

It builds deeper understanding, insight, and long-term strategic thinking.

Education is especially important for executives, system owners, data owners, managers, security leaders, and decision-makers.

### Security Training

Training focuses on **how** to perform security-related tasks.

It builds practical knowledge and skills.

Training is especially important for IT teams, administrators, developers, system custodians, security analysts, and privileged users.

### Security Awareness

Awareness focuses on **what** people need to recognize and remember in their daily work.

It supports behavior change.

Awareness is especially important for all employees, contractors, and users.

In simple terms:

**Education explains why.**
**Training teaches how.**
**Awareness reinforces what.**

This distinction is important because different audiences need different learning approaches.

NIST SP 800-50 Rev. 1 provides updated guidance for developing and managing a cybersecurity and privacy learning program and emphasizes a lifecycle approach to building such programs. ([NIST Computer Security Resource Center][1])

## Why SETA Matters

Many security incidents are not caused by highly sophisticated attacks.

They often begin with everyday situations:

- A user clicks on a phishing email
- A privileged account is misused
- Sensitive data is stored in the wrong location
- A developer introduces insecure code
- A manager approves risky access
- An employee uses an unmanaged AI tool with confidential information
- A contractor does not understand reporting procedures

Technology can reduce risk, but it cannot replace informed and responsible behavior.

A good SETA program helps organizations:

- Reduce human-related security incidents
- Improve reporting of suspicious activity
- Strengthen compliance
- Support secure business processes
- Improve cybersecurity culture
- Enable better decision-making
- Reduce risk across departments

The original NIST SP 800-50 described key lifecycle activities for building an IT security awareness and training program, including program design, material development, implementation, and post-implementation activities. ([NIST][2])

## Education, Training, and Awareness: The Key Differences

A common mistake is to treat all security learning as “awareness training.”

That is too simplistic.

A short phishing video may help general users recognize suspicious emails, but it will not prepare a system administrator to configure secure logging.

A hands-on lab may train an engineer, but it may not help executives understand cyber risk in business terms.

Each SETA component has a different purpose.

### Education

**Attribute:** Why  
**Level:** Insight  
**Objective:** Understanding  
**Typical methods:** Discussion, seminar, reading, executive briefings  
**Test measures:** Essays, discussions, scenarios, strategic decisions  
**Impact timeframe:** Long-term  

### Training

**Attribute:** How  
**Level:** Knowledge  
**Objective:** Skill  
**Typical methods:** Lectures, case studies, labs, hands-on exercises  
**Test measures:** Problem solving, practical demonstrations  
**Impact timeframe:** Intermediate  

### Awareness

**Attribute:** What  
**Level:** Information  
**Objective:** Behavior  
**Typical methods:** Interactive content, videos, posters, games, reminders  
**Test measures:** Quizzes, true/false questions, multiple choice  
**Impact timeframe:** Short-term  

This model helps organizations avoid a one-size-fits-all approach.

## Practical Example: Phishing

Phishing is a good example to explain the difference between education, training, and awareness.

### Awareness

A general employee receives short messages such as:

- Do not click suspicious links
- Check the sender address
- Report suspicious emails
- Be careful with urgent requests

This creates basic recognition.

### Training

A help desk employee receives more detailed training:

- How to identify phishing indicators
- How to analyze email headers
- How to escalate suspected phishing emails
- How to support users who reported suspicious messages

This builds operational skill.

### Education

Executives learn:

- Why phishing is a business risk
- How phishing can lead to ransomware
- Why security culture matters
- How leadership behavior influences employee behavior
- How phishing risk connects to financial, legal, and reputational impact

This builds strategic understanding.

Same topic.

Different audience.

Different learning objective.

## Role-Based SETA: One Message Does Not Fit Everyone

A mature SETA program must be **role-based**.

Different roles create different risks and require different knowledge.

### Executive

Executives need education focused on:

- Strategy
- Policy
- Organizational impact
- Risk appetite
- Regulatory exposure
- Business resilience

They do not need deep technical configuration training.

They need to understand cyber risk as business risk.

### System Owner

System owners need education focused on:

- Business process impact
- System criticality
- Risk ownership
- Recovery priorities
- Control accountability

They must understand how security affects the business process they own.

### Data Owner

Data owners need education focused on:

- Data protection
- Privacy
- Classification
- Legal obligations
- Access decisions
- Data retention

They must understand what data they own and how it must be protected.

### Custodian

Custodians need training focused on:

- Implementing controls
- Managing systems
- Monitoring security configurations
- Applying patches
- Reviewing logs
- Maintaining technical safeguards

They need hands-on operational skills.

### Privileged User

Privileged users need training focused on:

- Administrative responsibility
- Secure use of privileged accounts
- Least privilege
- Just-in-time access
- Session monitoring
- Credential protection

A privileged user can create significant risk through a single mistake.

### General User

General users need awareness focused on:

- Responsible use
- Acceptable Use Policy
- Password hygiene
- MFA
- Phishing
- Social engineering
- Reporting suspicious activity
- Data handling

Users must understand that individual actions matter.

## Practical Example: Role-Based Ransomware Preparedness

A ransomware scenario requires different SETA activities for different roles.

### Executives

They need to understand:

- Business impact of downtime
- Decision-making during crisis
- Communication responsibilities
- Risk acceptance
- Investment priorities

### IT Administrators

They need to know:

- Backup restoration steps
- Network isolation procedures
- Log collection
- Endpoint containment
- Recovery sequencing

### Help Desk

They need to recognize:

- Early signs of compromise
- User reports that may indicate ransomware
- Escalation procedures
- Communication scripts

### Employees

They need to know:

- How to identify phishing attempts
- How to report suspicious emails
- What not to do during an incident
- Why quick reporting matters

A strong SETA program prepares each group for its role before the incident happens.

## Executive Continuing Education

Executives require a special approach.

The objective is not to turn executives into technical specialists.

The objective is to create **insight and understanding for long-term decision-making**.

Executive education should:

- Use business language
- Be specific to institutional goals
- Include regulatory and strategic context
- Focus on a limited number of topics
- Provide a discussion framework
- Include pre-reading materials when useful
- Respect limited time
- Avoid unnecessary technical depth

### Practical Example: Board-Level Cyber Risk Session

A strong executive session could focus on:

- Current threat landscape
- Top business-critical cyber risks
- Ransomware impact scenarios
- Supply chain dependencies
- Cyber insurance limitations
- Regulatory exposure
- Investment priorities
- Incident communication responsibilities

The key is to translate cybersecurity into business impact.

Instead of saying:

“EDR coverage is incomplete.”

Say:

“Several business-critical endpoints are not covered by detection capabilities, which increases the risk that ransomware activity may remain undetected during early stages.”

That is the language executives need.

## Security Awareness Programs

Security awareness programs should include **employees and contractors**.

They should not be limited to permanent staff.

Contractors, temporary workers, external consultants, third-party support teams, and outsourced service providers may all have access to systems, data, or business processes.

A strong security awareness program should include:

- Onboarding before access is granted
- Post-boarding reinforcement
- Annual compliance training
- Hot-topic awareness campaigns
- Refresher programs
- Instructor-led sessions
- Recorded training
- Online modules
- Videos and demonstrations
- Interactive quizzes
- Ongoing reminders
- Posters, games, contests, surveys
- Situational awareness communications

### Practical Example: Onboarding Before Access

Before a new employee receives access to internal systems, they complete a short onboarding module covering:

- Acceptable use
- Password and MFA requirements
- Data classification
- Phishing reporting
- Use of approved tools
- Clean desk expectations
- Incident reporting channels

This ensures that access is granted only after the person understands basic responsibilities.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

## Practical Example: Situational Awareness Communication

A new phishing campaign targets employees with fake Microsoft 365 login pages.

Instead of waiting for the next annual training cycle, the security team sends a short situational awareness message:

- What the campaign looks like
- What users should avoid
- How to report it
- Who to contact
- A screenshot example
- A reminder not to enter credentials after clicking email links

This is practical, timely, and relevant.

Awareness must be continuous, not only annual.

## SETA Techniques That Work

A modern SETA program should use multiple techniques.

Different people learn differently.

Different risks require different formats.

### 1. Gamification

Gamification uses elements such as competition, achievement, progress, points, badges, or leaderboards.

It can increase motivation and engagement.

### Practical Example

A company creates a monthly “Security Challenge” where employees earn points for:

- Reporting phishing emails
- Completing micro-learning modules
- Identifying security mistakes in simulated scenarios
- Participating in quizzes

The goal is not entertainment alone.

The goal is engagement that leads to better behavior.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

## 2. Capture the Flag Challenges

Capture the Flag, or CTF, exercises provide hands-on learning.

They are especially useful for:

- Security teams
- Developers
- IT administrators
- Students
- Technical teams

### Practical Example

A developer-focused CTF includes challenges such as:

- Finding hardcoded secrets
- Identifying SQL injection
- Fixing insecure authentication logic
- Understanding insecure file uploads
- Reviewing vulnerable code

This teaches secure development more effectively than slides alone.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

## 3. Phishing Simulations

Phishing simulations provide a safe way to test users and provide feedback.

They should be designed carefully.

The goal should be learning, not embarrassment.

### Practical Example

A phishing simulation is sent to employees.

Users who click are redirected to a short explanation page showing:

- Which indicators were suspicious
- What they should have checked
- How to report the email
- Why the attack technique works

Metrics are tracked over time, but individuals are not publicly shamed.

This supports learning and trust.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

## 4. Computer-Based Training

Computer-based training can provide interactive and scalable learning.

It is useful for:

- Annual compliance training
- Refresher courses
- Role-based modules
- Short micro-learning sessions
- Remote employees

### Practical Example

A 10-minute module teaches employees how to handle sensitive documents.

It includes:

- A short video
- Interactive examples
- A scenario-based quiz
- Immediate feedback
- Completion tracking

This is simple, measurable, and scalable.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

## 5. Role-Based Training

Role-based training teaches people what they specifically need to know based on their role.

### Practical Example

A privileged user training module includes:

- Secure admin workstation usage
- Password vaulting
- MFA requirements
- Avoiding shared admin accounts
- Logging and monitoring expectations
- Change approval requirements
- Emergency access procedures

This training is far more relevant than generic awareness content.

## SETA Maturity: From Ad Hoc to Metrics-Driven

A SETA program should mature over time.

A useful maturity path could look like this:

### 1. Non-Existent

There is no structured awareness, training, or education program.

Security learning happens randomly or not at all.

### 2. Ad Hoc

Training exists, but it is inconsistent.

Some teams receive training, others do not.

Content may be outdated.

Metrics are limited.

### 3. Compliance Driven

Training is performed mainly to satisfy regulatory or audit requirements.

Employees complete annual modules, but behavior change is limited.

### 4. Integrated

SETA is integrated into onboarding, role changes, incident response, leadership communication, and business processes.

Different roles receive different learning paths.

### 5. Measurable and Reportable

The program is metrics-driven.

Awareness, behavior, and culture are measured and reported.

Security learning is continuously improved based on evidence.

The goal should be to move beyond “everyone completed training” toward measurable improvement in behavior and culture.

## SETA Metrics: What Should Be Measured?

A mature SETA program measures more than completion rates.

The primary measurement areas are:

- Awareness
- Behavior
- Culture

### Awareness Metrics

Awareness measures participation, knowledge, and comprehension.

Examples:

- Number of participants
- Completion rates
- Quiz results
- Knowledge checks
- Diversity of awareness activities
- Participation by department or role

### Behavioral Metrics

Behavior measures response to simulated or actual events.

Examples:

- Phishing simulation click rates
- Phishing reporting rates
- Suspicious activity reporting
- Clean desk compliance
- Incident reporting trends
- Attempts to visit blocked websites
- Use of approved reporting channels

### Culture Metrics

Culture measures activity, attitude, and engagement.

Examples:

- Security topics in team meetings
- Voluntary participation in security events
- Survey results
- Security champions participation
- Internal community engagement
- Management support
- Employee willingness to report mistakes

The strongest SETA programs measure whether people behave differently after learning.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

## Practical Example: Measuring Phishing Resilience

A company runs phishing simulations every quarter.

At first, the click rate is 22%.

Only 4% of employees report suspicious emails.

After six months of targeted training:

- Click rate drops to 8%
- Reporting rate increases to 35%
- Help desk escalation improves
- Security team receives earlier warnings
- Incident response becomes faster

This is meaningful progress.

Not because people completed a module.

But because behavior changed.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

## Practical Example: Measuring Security Culture

A company introduces a security champions program.

Each department nominates one person who acts as a local contact for security topics.

After one year, the company measures:

- Number of departments with champions
- Number of questions raised before risky changes
- Participation in security events
- Feedback from departments
- Increase in early reporting
- Reduction in repeated policy violations

This shows whether security is becoming part of normal work.

## What Many SETA Programs Get Wrong

Many organizations make the same mistakes.

### 1. Treating SETA as Annual Compliance Only

Annual training is not enough.

Security behavior must be reinforced throughout the year.

### 2. Using the Same Training for Everyone

Executives, developers, administrators, and general users do not need the same content.

### 3. Measuring Completion Instead of Behavior

Completion does not prove understanding or behavior change.

### 4. Creating Fear Instead of Trust

Employees should feel safe reporting mistakes and suspicious activity.

A blame culture reduces transparency.

### 5. Ignoring Contractors and Third Parties

External users can create significant risk.

They must be included when they access systems or data.

### 6. Using Technical Language for Non-Technical Audiences

Awareness must be understandable.

Security teams should communicate in practical, business-oriented language.

### 7. Not Updating Content

Training must reflect current threats, tools, policies, and business processes.

## Practical Example: AI Awareness for Employees

Employees increasingly use AI tools to summarize text, generate emails, analyze data, or write code.

This creates new risks.

A practical AI awareness module should explain:

- Do not enter confidential data into unapproved AI tools
- Validate AI-generated outputs
- Be careful with hallucinations
- Do not use AI-generated code without review
- Follow company policy for approved AI tools
- Report risky or unexpected AI behavior

This is becoming essential.

AI awareness is now part of cybersecurity awareness.

## Practical Example: Developer Security Training

Developers need more than generic awareness.

They need training that connects security to their daily work.

A strong developer training program could include:

- OWASP Top 10
- Secure coding principles
- Authentication and authorization
- Input validation
- Secrets management
- Dependency security
- API security
- Threat modeling
- Secure CI/CD pipelines
- Code review practices
- AI-assisted coding risks

A practical lab might ask developers to fix insecure code in a controlled environment.

This builds skill, not just awareness.

## Practical Example: Privileged User Training

Privileged users represent high-risk roles.

Their training should include:

- Privileged access management
- Avoiding shared accounts
- Admin session logging
- Secure remote administration
- Emergency access procedures
- Separation of duties
- Change management
- Phishing risks for administrators
- Protecting administrative credentials

A single compromised admin account can lead to major business impact.

That is why privileged users need targeted training.

## Building a SETA Program: A Practical Roadmap

A structured SETA program can follow this roadmap:

### Step 1: Define Objectives

Clarify what the program should achieve.

Examples:

- Reduce phishing clicks
- Increase reporting rates
- Improve data handling
- Support compliance
- Strengthen security culture
- Improve role-based security skills

### Step 2: Identify Audiences

Define target groups.

Examples:

- Executives
- Managers
- General users
- Developers
- IT administrators
- Privileged users
- Contractors
- Data owners
- System owners

### Step 3: Map Topics to Roles

Do not deliver everything to everyone.

Create role-based learning paths.

### Step 4: Select Learning Methods

Use a mix of:

- Videos
- Workshops
- Labs
- Simulations
- Micro-learning
- CTFs
- Quizzes
- Posters
- Campaigns
- Executive briefings

### Step 5: Implement Continuously

Spread learning across the year.

Avoid a once-a-year-only approach.

### Step 6: Measure Effectiveness

Measure awareness, behavior, and culture.

### Step 7: Improve the Program

Use metrics, incidents, feedback, and threat intelligence to update the program.

## Final Thoughts

Security Education, Training, and Awareness is not a side activity.

It is a strategic capability.

A mature SETA program helps organizations move from:

- Information to understanding
- Understanding to skill
- Skill to behavior
- Behavior to culture

Education explains why security matters.

Training teaches how to perform security tasks.

Awareness reinforces what people need to recognize and do every day.

The most effective cybersecurity programs do not rely only on technology.

They build people, processes, and culture around security.

Because in the end, cybersecurity is not only about systems.

It is about decisions, habits, responsibility, and trust.
